Introduction

Who was Hanna Lindh?

Between 1879 and 1881, Hanna Lindh and her family had Pensionat Granparken built above Societetsparken in Norrtälje. The first guests moved in in 1883, and the guesthouse became part of the town's emergence as a seaside resort and summer destination. But who was the woman behind it all? And why has her story almost been forgotten?

Hanna's House is the story of a person, a house, and a Norrtälje in transformation.

The person behind it all

Hanna Lindh — the person behind it all

Early feministActivistEntrepreneurCommunity builder

Hanna Lindh (1839–1916) was an early feminist and activist who combined female entrepreneurship with social change.

In the winter of 1885, Hanna lost her third son to illness and entered a personal crisis that led her to think in radically different ways. She questioned her Christian faith and the bourgeois ideal of family life, and began to connect illness with class and living conditions.

In the spring of 1886, she founded the Salvation Army in Norrtälje together with a group of like-minded companions and began organizing those around her to change society. The guesthouse, which she had established for society visitors a few years earlier, now became a meeting place for people with visions and ideas about a different way of life.

Hanna put action before faith. Dressed in a black uniform with red details, she used her voice and body as an activist in the street. In the summer of 1888, she worked with the Stockholm Malt Cure Institute to help women, children and struggling poor families in particular. The following year, she collaborated with Karlberg Palace in Stockholm on matters relating to defence and military preparedness.

She sought to unite people with differing ideas and developed her own view of how society should be governed and understood — as part of a broader ideological project.

Hanna Lindh and Lindh's Guesthouse, now known as Pensionat Granparken, form an important piece of the story of the Salvation Army's foundation, female entrepreneurship and the development of tourism in Norrtälje around the turn of the twentieth century.

Hanna was driven by real change. On 15 June 1888, she placed the following advertisement in Norrtelje Tidning:

“Norrtelje Malt Cure Institute, a branch of the Stockholm Malt Cure Institute, will open at the Sanatorium in Granparken, house no. 26, on Monday the 18th of this June. Freshly prepared warm malt infusion will be available to the esteemed public daily from 9 a.m. to 2 p.m., for drinking on the premises or collection. According to notices posted at the Bathhouse and the Society, as well as medical certificates available at the institute, the malt cure is particularly effective for chlorosis, anaemia and general weakness, threatened consumption, and catarrh of the throat, lungs, stomach and intestines, among other illnesses. The price is 12 kronor per month, or 15 kronor for home delivery; for children, 10 kronor; a discount is available for family tickets. Doctors A. Brandt and Y. Ljungberg have kindly undertaken to provide further information. Norrtälje, 15 June 1888. /The superintendent of the institute.”

Norrtelje Tidning, 15 June 1888

Pensionat Granparken

A guesthouse from 1879–1881

Pensionat Granparken was built between 1879 and 1881 during Norrtälje's golden age as a spa town. The first guests moved in in 1883. Its position above Societetsparken made the guesthouse a natural part of the bathing guests' lives. The name came from Granparken — the landscaped park where visitors strolled for their health.

New Research

When did the story of Granparken really begin?

Through a study of contemporary sources, property records, family documents and Ernst Lindh's memoirs, a clearer history emerges. Hanna's House presents new research suggesting that the building was raised between 1879 and 1881 and that the first guests moved in in 1883.

About the author

Fredrik Anthony

Fredrik Anthony grew up in Norrtälje and has worked for many years with art, cultural history and antiques.

Through Ernst Lindh's memoirs and his own research, he has rediscovered the story of Hanna Lindh and Pensionat Granparken — the first cohesive portrait of the woman behind the house.
